# Mediocre

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˌmiːdiˈoʊkər/
- **Simplified:** mee-dee-OH-kur

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Of only moderate or average quality; neither good nor bad, and often somewhat disappointing.
2. Not satisfactory; rather poor or second-rate.

## Etymology
From Latin *mediocris* ("of middle height, in a middle state, moderate, ordinary"), from *medius* ("middle") + *ocris* ("jagged mountain, peak"). The literal meaning was "halfway up the mountain" -- suggesting something that has not reached the summit of excellence. Entered English in the late 16th century through French *mediocre*.

## Usage Examples
1. "Despite the enormous budget, the film received mediocre reviews from critics and failed to generate strong box office returns." -- *Variety*
2. "Students who produce mediocre work are often capable of much more but lack the motivation to reach their full potential."
3. "The restaurant's food was mediocre at best, with bland flavors and unimaginative presentation."
4. "A culture that rewards mediocre performance discourages truly talented individuals from striving for excellence."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** average, ordinary, middling, passable, undistinguished, unremarkable, indifferent, second-rate, lackluster
**Antonyms:** excellent, outstanding, exceptional, superior, remarkable, superb, first-rate, extraordinary

## TOEFL Tips
- Often appears in reading passages about education, performance evaluation, and quality standards.
- Carries a mildly negative connotation -- it implies disappointment or that something falls short of expectations.
- In vocabulary questions, "mediocre" is typically tested as a synonym for "average" or "unremarkable."
- The noun "mediocrity" frequently appears in discussions about educational standards or cultural values.
- Useful in TOEFL writing when arguing that something is inadequate: "Settling for mediocre results undermines long-term academic success."
- The etymology (halfway up the mountain) provides a memorable image for recalling the meaning.
