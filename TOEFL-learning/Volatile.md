# Volatile

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˈvɒl.ə.taɪl/ (British) or /ˈvɑː.lə.t̬əl/ (American)
- Simplified: VOL-uh-tyl (British) or VOL-uh-tuhl (American)

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Liable to change rapidly and unpredictably, especially for the worse.
2. (Of a person) Easily aroused to strong emotions; temperamental.
3. (Chemistry) Evaporating rapidly at normal temperatures and pressures.
4. (Computing) Of memory: losing its contents when the power supply is interrupted.

## Etymology
From French *volatil*, from Latin *volatilis* ("flying, swift, transitory"), from *volatus*, past participle of *volare* ("to fly"). Entered English in the early 17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Financial markets have become increasingly volatile amid rising geopolitical tensions and inflationary pressures." -- *Financial Times*
2. "The volatile political situation in the region has made foreign investment extremely risky." -- *The Economist*
3. "Volatile organic compounds released by industrial facilities contribute significantly to air pollution." -- *Nature*
4. "Oil prices are notoriously volatile, subject to rapid swings driven by supply disruptions and shifting demand."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** unstable, unpredictable, changeable, erratic, turbulent, mercurial, capricious
**Antonyms:** stable, steady, constant, predictable, calm, settled

## Word Family
- **Noun:** volatility
- **Adverb:** (none standard)

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently appears in passages about economics, finance, and political science.
- The chemistry meaning (readily evaporating) may appear in science-related passages.
- "Volatility" is a key noun form often used in discussions of financial markets and climate.
- Understanding both the figurative (unstable) and literal (evaporating) meanings is important for vocabulary-in-context questions.
