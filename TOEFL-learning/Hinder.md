# Hinder

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈhɪndər/
- **Simplified:** HIN-dur

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To create difficulties for someone or something, resulting in delay or obstruction; to impede progress.
2. To prevent or restrict someone from doing something.

**Adjective** (archaic/rare)
1. Situated at the back; rear (e.g., "hinder parts").

## Etymology
From Old English *hindrian* ("to hold back, to delay, to impede"), from Proto-Germanic *hinderojan*, related to *hinder* ("behind"). The word is cognate with German *hindern* ("to prevent"). The root is related to "behind" and "hind" (rear). It has been in continuous use in English since before the 12th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Lack of adequate funding continues to hinder scientific research in many developing countries." -- *Nature*
2. "The severe weather conditions hindered rescue operations in the aftermath of the earthquake."
3. "Excessive regulation can hinder economic growth by discouraging entrepreneurship and innovation."
4. "The language barrier hindered communication between the medical team and the local population."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** impede, obstruct, hamper, inhibit, restrict, delay, block, thwart, retard, encumber
**Antonyms:** help, assist, facilitate, aid, promote, advance, encourage, expedite

## TOEFL Tips
- One of the most common verbs in TOEFL reading passages about obstacles to progress or development.
- Often tested alongside related words "impede," "hamper," and "obstruct" -- they are near-synonyms.
- Frequently appears in cause-and-effect passages explaining why something failed or was delayed.
- The noun form "hindrance" is also important: "Poverty is a major hindrance to educational attainment."
- In TOEFL writing, use "hinder" to discuss factors that prevent success: "Inadequate infrastructure hinders economic development in rural areas."
