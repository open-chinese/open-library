# Propensity

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /prəˈpɛn.sɪ.ti/
- **Simplified:** pruh-PEN-suh-tee

## Definition
**Noun**
1. A natural inclination or tendency to behave in a particular way
2. A disposition or predisposition toward a certain kind of behavior or action

## Etymology
From Latin *propensus*, past participle of *propendere* ("to hang forward, incline toward"), from *pro-* ("forward") + *pendere* ("to hang, weigh"). First used in English in the 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Studies have shown that children raised in bilingual households have a propensity for learning additional languages more quickly." — *Journal of Cognitive Development*
2. "The species has a well-documented propensity to migrate to warmer climates during the winter months." — *National Geographic*
3. "Economists noted the consumer's propensity to save rather than spend during periods of economic uncertainty." — *The Economist*
4. "His propensity for risk-taking ultimately defined his career as an entrepreneur." — *Forbes*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** tendency, inclination, predisposition, proclivity, penchant, leaning, predilection
**Antonyms:** aversion, disinclination, reluctance, antipathy, unwillingness

## Word Family
- **Propense** (adjective, archaic): inclined or disposed toward
- **Propend** (verb, rare): to lean toward or be inclined

## Collocations
- propensity for
- propensity to (do something)
- natural propensity
- human propensity
- marginal propensity (economics term)
- propensity toward

## TOEFL Tips
"Propensity" is commonly encountered in TOEFL reading passages dealing with psychology, economics, and behavioral science. It is often used to describe tendencies in human or animal behavior. In the reading section, you may be asked to identify it as a synonym for "tendency" or "inclination." In economics-related passages, look for phrases such as "marginal propensity to consume" or "propensity to save." The word signals a natural or inherent behavioral pattern rather than a deliberate choice.
