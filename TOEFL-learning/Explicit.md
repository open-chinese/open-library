# Explicit

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ɪkˈsplɪsɪt/
- **Simplified:** ik-SPLIS-it

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Stated clearly and in detail, leaving no room for confusion or doubt.
2. (Of a person) saying or expressing something directly and openly; forthright.
3. Describing or depicting sexual or violent content in a graphic, detailed manner.

## Etymology
From Latin *explicitus*, past participle of *explicare* ("to unfold, to unravel, to explain"), from *ex-* ("out") + *plicare* ("to fold"). Originally entered English in the 17th century meaning "unfolded" or "fully developed." The idea is that something explicit has been "unfolded" so that nothing remains hidden. Related to "explain," "explicate," "implicit," and "complicate."

## Usage Examples
1. "The contract contains explicit provisions regarding the responsibilities of each party involved." -- *Harvard Law Review*
2. "The professor gave explicit instructions for how the research paper should be formatted and submitted."
3. "While the regulations are explicit about what is prohibited, they are less clear about what is permitted."
4. "The author's implicit criticism in the first chapter becomes explicit in the final pages of the novel."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** clear, direct, specific, unambiguous, definite, express, precise, overt, unequivocal
**Antonyms:** implicit, vague, ambiguous, unclear, indirect, implied, tacit, subtle

## TOEFL Tips
- The explicit/implicit contrast is one of the most important distinctions in TOEFL reading comprehension.
- TOEFL reading questions frequently ask about "explicitly stated" information vs. information that is "implied" or "inferred."
- The phrase "The author explicitly states..." is standard TOEFL question language.
- Understanding this word helps with all "factual information" questions, which ask about what is explicitly mentioned in a passage.
- In TOEFL writing, being explicit (clear and direct) in your thesis and supporting arguments will earn higher scores.
