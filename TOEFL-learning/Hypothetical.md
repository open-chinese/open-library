# Hypothetical

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˌhaɪ.pəˈθet.ɪ.kəl/
- Simplified: hy-puh-THET-ih-kuhl

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Of, based on, or serving as a hypothesis; supposed but not necessarily real or true.
2. Imagined or suggested as a basis for reasoning or argument, without any assumption of its truth.

**Noun**
3. A hypothetical proposition or statement; a supposition.

## Etymology
From Greek *hypothetikos* ("assumed, supposed"), from *hypothesis* ("a placing under, foundation, supposition"), from *hypo-* ("under") + *thesis* ("a placing, proposition"), from *tithenai* ("to place"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The professor posed a hypothetical scenario to illustrate the ethical dilemma facing policymakers." -- *The New York Times*
2. "In a hypothetical world without fossil fuels, renewable energy would have developed far earlier." -- *The Economist*
3. "The study relies on hypothetical models that may not fully capture the complexity of real-world conditions." -- *Nature*
4. "Philosophers have long debated hypothetical questions about free will and determinism."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** theoretical, supposed, conjectural, speculative, assumed, imaginary, notional
**Antonyms:** actual, real, factual, proven, confirmed, empirical, demonstrated

## Word Family
- **Noun:** hypothesis (plural: hypotheses)
- **Verb:** hypothesize
- **Adverb:** hypothetically

## Collocations
- hypothetical scenario/situation
- hypothetical question
- purely/entirely hypothetical
- hypothetical example/case
- hypothetical model

## TOEFL Tips
- The entire word family (hypothesis, hypothesize, hypothetical, hypothetically) is extremely common on the TOEFL.
- "Hypothesis" frequently appears in science passages describing the scientific method.
- Distinguishing between "hypothetical" and "empirical" is a key academic skill tested on the TOEFL.
- In listening sections, professors often use hypothetical scenarios to explain abstract concepts.
