# Contingent

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /kənˈtɪn.dʒənt/
- **Simplified:** kun-TIN-junt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Dependent on or conditioned by something else; subject to chance
2. Occurring or existing only if certain conditions are met; conditional
3. (Philosophy) Not logically necessary; true only under existing conditions

**Noun**
1. A group of people sharing a common feature, forming part of a larger group
2. A body of troops or representatives sent to join a larger force

## Etymology
From Latin *contingentem*, present participle of *contingere* ("to touch, befall, happen"), from *con-* ("together with") + *tangere* ("to touch"). Entered English in the late 14th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Funding for the project is contingent upon approval from the board of directors." — *The Wall Street Journal*
2. "A large contingent of international observers was dispatched to monitor the election." — *The New York Times*
3. "The success of the conservation effort is contingent on the cooperation of local communities." — *Conservation Biology*
4. "The company made a contingent offer of employment, pending the results of the background check." — *Harvard Business Review*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms (adjective):** conditional, dependent, subject to, reliant on, provisional
**Synonyms (noun):** group, delegation, detachment, party, deputation
**Antonyms (adjective):** unconditional, absolute, certain, guaranteed, independent

## Word Family
- **Contingency** (noun): a future event or circumstance that is possible but cannot be predicted with certainty; a provision for such an event
- **Contingently** (adverb): in a contingent manner

## Collocations
- contingent on/upon
- contingent liability
- contingent workforce
- contingent offer
- contingency plan
- large contingent

## TOEFL Tips
"Contingent" is a versatile TOEFL word that appears as both an adjective and a noun. As an adjective, it means "dependent on" and often appears in the construction "contingent on/upon." As a noun, it refers to a group of people. In reading passages, the adjective form is more commonly tested, typically in contexts about conditions that must be met. The related noun "contingency" (as in "contingency plan") also appears frequently. Understanding the conditional nature of this word is key -- it always implies that an outcome depends on something else occurring first.
