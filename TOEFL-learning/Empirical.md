# Empirical

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ɪmˈpɪr.ɪ.kəl/
- Simplified: im-PEER-ih-kuhl

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Based on, concerned with, or verifiable by observation or experience rather than theory or pure logic.
2. Derived from or guided by experience or experiment rather than abstract reasoning.

## Etymology
From Latin *empiricus*, from Greek *empeirikos* ("experienced"), from *empeiria* ("experience"), from *en-* ("in") + *peira* ("trial, experiment"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The researchers presented empirical evidence that contradicted the long-held theoretical model." -- *Nature*
2. "Without empirical data to support the claim, the hypothesis remains purely speculative." -- *The Economist*
3. "The study is one of the first to provide empirical support for the link between sleep deprivation and cognitive decline." -- *The Lancet*
4. "Empirical research in the social sciences often relies on surveys, experiments, and observational studies."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** observational, experiential, experimental, evidence-based, factual, practical
**Antonyms:** theoretical, hypothetical, speculative, abstract, conjectural

## Word Family
- **Noun:** empiricism, empiricist
- **Adverb:** empirically

## Collocations
- empirical evidence/data
- empirical research/study
- empirical analysis/investigation
- empirical support/basis
- empirical observation/findings

## TOEFL Tips
- One of the most important academic vocabulary words for the TOEFL, especially in science-related passages.
- Frequently contrasted with "theoretical" or "hypothetical" in academic discussions.
- Understanding this word is critical for passages about the scientific method and research methodology.
- In listening sections, professors often emphasize the importance of "empirical evidence" when discussing research findings.
