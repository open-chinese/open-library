# Skeptical

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˈskep.tɪ.kəl/
- Simplified: SKEP-tih-kuhl
- Also spelled: sceptical (British English)

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Not easily convinced; having doubts or reservations.
2. Relating to the philosophical doctrine that true knowledge or certain knowledge is impossible.

## Etymology
From Greek *skeptikos* ("inquiring, reflective"), from *skeptesthai* ("to look carefully, consider"), related to *skopos* ("one who watches, a target"). Entered English in the late 16th century through Latin *scepticus*.

## Usage Examples
1. "Investors remain skeptical about the company's ability to meet its ambitious growth targets." -- *Financial Times*
2. "Many scientists are skeptical of the study's findings, citing methodological flaws in the research design." -- *Nature*
3. "The public has grown increasingly skeptical of government claims in the wake of repeated scandals." -- *The New York Times*
4. "While some hailed the new treatment as a breakthrough, medical experts urged a more skeptical evaluation of the evidence." -- *The Lancet*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** doubtful, dubious, unconvinced, questioning, incredulous, distrustful, cynical
**Antonyms:** credulous, trusting, gullible, believing, convinced, naive

## Word Family
- **Noun:** skeptic (person), skepticism (philosophy/attitude)
- **Adverb:** skeptically

## Collocations
- remain/be skeptical
- skeptical of/about (something)
- highly/deeply skeptical
- healthy skepticism
- skeptical observer/critic

## TOEFL Tips
- Common in reading passages about scientific debates, public policy, and media literacy.
- TOEFL listening lectures frequently feature speakers expressing skepticism about certain theories or findings.
- "Skepticism" as a philosophical concept may appear in passages about the history of science or epistemology.
- In the speaking and writing sections, expressing appropriate skepticism demonstrates critical thinking skills valued on the TOEFL.
