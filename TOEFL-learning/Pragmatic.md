# Pragmatic

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /præɡˈmæt.ɪk/
- Simplified: prag-MAT-ik

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Dealing with things sensibly and realistically in a way that is based on practical rather than theoretical considerations.
2. Relating to philosophical pragmatism.

## Etymology
From Greek *pragmatikos* ("fit for business, active"), from *pragma* ("deed, act"), from *prassein* ("to do, to act"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The administration adopted a pragmatic approach to foreign policy, prioritizing achievable goals over ideological purity." -- *The New York Times*
2. "Rather than debating abstract principles, the committee took a pragmatic stance on budget allocation." -- *The Economist*
3. "Pragmatic leaders understand that compromise is often necessary to achieve lasting reform."
4. "The company's pragmatic decision to pivot its business model saved it from bankruptcy."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** practical, realistic, sensible, down-to-earth, matter-of-fact, utilitarian
**Antonyms:** idealistic, impractical, unrealistic, theoretical, utopian

## Word Family
- **Noun:** pragmatism, pragmatist
- **Adverb:** pragmatically

## Collocations
- pragmatic approach
- pragmatic solution
- pragmatic decision
- pragmatic leader
- pragmatic view/perspective

## TOEFL Tips
- Commonly appears in reading passages about politics, business strategy, and decision-making.
- Often contrasted with "idealistic" or "theoretical" in TOEFL texts.
- Useful in the independent writing task when arguing for practical solutions.
- In listening sections, professors may describe pragmatic approaches to problem-solving.
