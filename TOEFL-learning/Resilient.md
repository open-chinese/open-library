# Resilient

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /rɪˈzɪl.i.ənt/
- Simplified: rih-ZIL-ee-uhnt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Able to withstand or recover quickly from difficult conditions; tough and adaptable.
2. (Of a substance or object) Able to spring back into shape after being bent, stretched, or compressed; elastic.

## Etymology
From Latin *resiliens*, present participle of *resilire* ("to spring back, rebound"), from *re-* ("back") + *salire* ("to jump, leap"). Entered English in the mid-17th century, originally in the physical sense.

## Usage Examples
1. "Communities that invest in infrastructure and emergency preparedness tend to be more resilient in the face of natural disasters." -- *The New York Times*
2. "Despite the economic downturn, the labor market has proven remarkably resilient." -- *The Economist*
3. "Coral reefs, once considered resilient ecosystems, are now showing alarming signs of irreversible damage." -- *Nature*
4. "The most resilient organizations are those that can adapt quickly to changing market conditions." -- *Harvard Business Review*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** tough, hardy, adaptable, flexible, durable, robust, buoyant, strong
**Antonyms:** fragile, vulnerable, weak, brittle, delicate, susceptible

## Word Family
- **Noun:** resilience, resiliency
- **Adverb:** resiliently

## Collocations
- resilient economy/community
- remarkably/highly resilient
- resilient to (change/stress)
- build/become resilient
- resilient infrastructure/system

## TOEFL Tips
- Increasingly common in academic discourse about climate change, economics, psychology, and public health.
- "Resilience" has become a key concept in multiple academic fields, making it likely to appear across TOEFL topics.
- Often appears in passages about how organisms, communities, or systems respond to stress and change.
