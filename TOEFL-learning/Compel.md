# Compel

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /kəmˈpɛl/
- **Simplified:** kum-PEL

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To force or oblige someone to do something; to make an action necessary or unavoidable.
2. To bring about something by the use of pressure, authority, or irresistible force.
3. To drive or urge with force; to constrain.

## Etymology
From Latin *compellere* ("to drive together, to force"), from *com-* ("together") + *pellere* ("to drive"). Related to "compulsion," "compulsory," and "propel." Entered English in the 14th century through Old French.

## Usage Examples
1. "The new evidence **compelled** the court to reopen the investigation." — *The Washington Post*
2. "Economic necessity **compelled** many rural families to migrate to urban centers in search of employment." — *The Economist*
3. "The law **compels** employers to provide a safe working environment for all staff members." — *Harvard Law Review*
4. "Her passionate argument **compelled** the audience to reconsider their assumptions about climate policy."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** force, oblige, coerce, require, impel, constrain, pressure, mandate, necessitate
**Antonyms:** deter, discourage, dissuade, prevent, allow, permit, liberate

- **circumstances** / **evidence** compel

## TOEFL Tips
- Often appears in **history and social science** passages where laws, social pressures, or events force people to act.
- The adjective form "compelling" (meaning "convincing" or "irresistible") is even more frequent on the TOEFL. A "compelling argument" means a very persuasive one.
- In vocabulary-in-context questions, "compel" is often tested alongside synonyms like "force" or "oblige." Note that "compel" implies stronger pressure than "encourage" or "persuade."
- Watch for the passive construction "be compelled to" in reading passages, which emphasizes the lack of choice.
