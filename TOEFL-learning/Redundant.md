# Redundant

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /rɪˈdʌn.dənt/
- **Simplified:** rih-DUN-dunt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. No longer needed or useful; superfluous
2. Containing more words or information than necessary; repetitive
3. (Engineering/Computing) Included as a backup or fail-safe in case of failure of the primary component
4. (British English) Made unemployed because one's job is no longer necessary

## Etymology
From Latin *redundantem*, present participle of *redundare* ("to overflow, be excessive"), from *re-* ("back, again") + *undare* ("to surge, flow"), from *unda* ("wave"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The editor removed several redundant paragraphs that repeated information already presented in the introduction." — *The Chicago Manual of Style*
2. "Modern aircraft are equipped with redundant navigation systems to ensure safety in case of equipment failure." — *Aviation Week*
3. "As automation increases, some traditional manufacturing roles are becoming redundant." — *The Economist*
4. "The researcher argued that the additional variable was redundant, as it provided no new explanatory power to the model." — *Journal of Statistical Analysis*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** superfluous, unnecessary, excessive, surplus, extraneous, repetitive, expendable
**Antonyms:** essential, necessary, vital, indispensable, crucial, required

## Word Family
- **Redundancy** (noun): the state of being no longer needed; unnecessary repetition
- **Redundantly** (adverb): in a redundant manner
- **Redundance** (noun, rare): redundancy

## Collocations
- redundant information
- redundant system
- make redundant
- redundant workers
- redundant data
- seemingly redundant

## TOEFL Tips
"Redundant" appears in TOEFL passages related to writing, technology, engineering, and economics. In reading comprehension, it is often tested as a vocabulary-in-context question where the answer is "unnecessary" or "superfluous." Be aware of the dual meaning: in everyday and academic contexts it means "unnecessarily repetitive," but in engineering and computing contexts it refers to backup systems, which is a positive attribute. The TOEFL writing section also tests your ability to avoid redundancy in your own essays.
