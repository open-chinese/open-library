# Analogous

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /əˈnæl.ə.ɡəs/
- **Simplified:** uh-NAL-uh-gus

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Comparable in certain respects, typically in a way that makes clearer the nature of the things compared
2. (Biology) Similar in function but different in evolutionary origin and structure

## Etymology
From Latin *analogus*, from Greek *analogos* ("proportionate"), from *ana-* ("upon, according to") + *logos* ("ratio, word"). Entered English in the mid-17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The relationship between a CEO and a corporation is analogous to that of a captain and a ship." — *Harvard Business Review*
2. "The wings of bats and the wings of insects are analogous structures -- similar in function but different in origin." — *Evolutionary Biology*
3. "The spread of misinformation online is analogous to the spread of an infectious disease through a population." — *The Atlantic*
4. "The process of natural selection is roughly analogous to artificial selection practiced by animal breeders." — *On the Origin of Species*, Charles Darwin

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** comparable, similar, parallel, equivalent, corresponding, akin, like
**Antonyms:** different, dissimilar, unlike, unrelated, disparate, contrasting

## Word Family
- **Analogy** (noun): a comparison between things that have similar features
- **Analogously** (adverb): in an analogous manner
- **Analogize** (verb): to make a comparison or draw an analogy
- **Analogical** (adjective): relating to or involving analogy

## Collocations
- analogous to
- analogous situation
- analogous structure (biology)
- roughly analogous
- directly analogous
- analogous process

## TOEFL Tips
"Analogous" is a high-frequency TOEFL word that appears in reading passages across science, social science, and humanities. It is especially common in biology passages discussing analogous versus homologous structures. In reading comprehension, you may be asked to identify what two things are being compared when the author uses "analogous." The word always implies similarity in one specific aspect, not overall identity. In the writing section, using analogies effectively (and using the word "analogous") can strengthen your arguments and demonstrate vocabulary range.
