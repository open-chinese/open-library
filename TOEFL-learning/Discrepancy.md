# Discrepancy

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /dɪˈskrɛp.ən.si/
- **Simplified:** dih-SKREP-un-see

## Definition
**Noun (countable)**
1. A lack of compatibility or similarity between two or more facts, figures, or accounts; an inconsistency.
2. A difference between things that should be the same or similar.
3. A divergence or disagreement between results, statements, or data.

## Etymology
From Latin *discrepantia* ("discordance, disagreement"), from *discrepare* ("to sound differently, to disagree"), from *dis-* ("apart") + *crepare* ("to rattle, creak, make noise"). The idea is of sounds that do not harmonize. Entered English in the early 17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Auditors discovered a significant **discrepancy** between the company's reported earnings and its actual revenue." — *The Wall Street Journal*
2. "The **discrepancy** between the results of the two studies prompted researchers to conduct a third investigation." — *Nature*
3. "There is a notable **discrepancy** in educational achievement between students from high-income and low-income families." — *The Economist*
4. "The **discrepancies** in witness testimonies raised doubts about the reliability of the evidence presented at trial."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** inconsistency, difference, disparity, divergence, disagreement, variance, mismatch, gap, contradiction
**Antonyms:** agreement, consistency, correspondence, accord, harmony, conformity

## Word Family

## Collocations
- **significant** / **notable** / **major** discrepancy
- discrepancy **between** X and Y
- discrepancy **in** the data / results / figures
- **account for** / **explain** / **resolve** a discrepancy
- **reveal** / **discover** / **identify** a discrepancy

## TOEFL Tips
- Appears in **reading and listening** sections, especially in passages about scientific research, statistics, and comparative studies.
- On the TOEFL, "discrepancy" often signals a problem, contradiction, or point of debate that the passage or lecture will explore further.
- Understanding "discrepancy" is important for **inference questions** where you must identify how two sets of data or viewpoints differ.
- The word often appears in academic discussions where researchers attempt to explain why two findings do not match.
- Be ready to identify what is being compared when you encounter "discrepancy" in a passage.
