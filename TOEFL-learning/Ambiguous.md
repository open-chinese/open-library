# Ambiguous

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /æmˈbɪɡ.ju.əs/
- Simplified: am-BIG-yoo-uhs

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Open to more than one interpretation; having a double meaning.
2. Unclear or inexact because a choice between alternatives has not been made.
3. Difficult to classify or understand; not having a clear meaning.

## Etymology
From Latin *ambiguus* ("having double meaning, shifting, uncertain"), from *ambigere* ("to go around, wander, doubt"), composed of *ambi-* ("both, around") + *agere* ("to drive, lead"). Entered English in the early 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The legislation's ambiguous wording has led to conflicting interpretations in lower courts." -- *The New York Times*
2. "The results of the study were ambiguous, leaving researchers unable to draw firm conclusions." -- *Nature*
3. "Her ambiguous response left everyone uncertain about whether she supported the proposal."
4. "The treaty's ambiguous language on territorial boundaries has been a source of diplomatic tension for decades." -- *The Economist*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** vague, unclear, equivocal, indeterminate, nebulous, cryptic
**Antonyms:** clear, unambiguous, definite, explicit, unequivocal, precise

## Word Family
- **Noun:** ambiguity (plural: ambiguities)
- **Adverb:** ambiguously
- **Verb:** (none standard; "disambiguate" is the related verb)

## Collocations
- ambiguous language/wording
- deliberately ambiguous
- morally ambiguous
- ambiguous results
- ambiguous meaning/interpretation

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently tested in reading comprehension questions about the meaning of specific passages.
- TOEFL reading passages may themselves contain ambiguous statements that require careful analysis.
- Understanding "ambiguity" is essential for inference questions where the answer is not directly stated.
- In the writing section, avoiding ambiguous phrasing strengthens your essay.
