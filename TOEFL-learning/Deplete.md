# Deplete

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /dɪˈpliːt/
- **Simplified:** dih-PLEET

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To use up or consume a large portion of a resource or supply, reducing it significantly.
2. To empty or exhaust a stock, reserve, or quantity.
3. To diminish greatly in quantity, content, or value.

## Etymology
From Latin *depletus*, past participle of *deplere* ("to empty out"), from *de-* ("down, away, completely") + *plere* ("to fill"). Related to "replete" (full) and "complete." Entered English in the early 19th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Overfishing has severely **depleted** fish stocks in the North Atlantic, threatening the marine food chain." — *National Geographic*
2. "The prolonged drought has **depleted** the region's groundwater reserves to dangerously low levels." — *Scientific American*
3. "Excessive use of antibiotics **depletes** beneficial bacteria in the gut, potentially leading to further health complications." — *The Lancet*
4. "The country's foreign currency reserves were rapidly **depleted** during the economic crisis."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** exhaust, drain, consume, diminish, reduce, empty, use up, expend
**Antonyms:** replenish, restore, refill, supplement, augment, restock, increase

- deplete **energy** / **funds** / **stocks**
- **natural resources** are depleted

## TOEFL Tips
- Extremely common in **environmental science and ecology** passages on the TOEFL, particularly in discussions about resource management, deforestation, and pollution.
- Often paired with "replenish" as an antonym on the exam. Understanding this contrast helps with vocabulary questions.
- The noun form "depletion" appears frequently, especially in the phrase "ozone depletion" or "resource depletion."
- In listening lectures, professors often use "deplete" when discussing unsustainable practices and their consequences. Listen for cause-and-effect structures.
