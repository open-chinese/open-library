# Perpetuate

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /pərˈpɛtʃ.u.eɪt/
- **Simplified:** pur-PECH-oo-ayt

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To cause something to continue indefinitely; to make something last or endure without interruption.
2. To preserve from extinction or oblivion; to keep alive or in existence.
3. To make something (often something negative, such as a stereotype or injustice) persist or recur.

## Etymology
From Latin *perpetuatus*, past participle of *perpetuare* ("to make perpetual"), from *perpetuus* ("continuous, uninterrupted"), from *per-* ("through, completely") + *petere* ("to seek, go toward"). Related to "perpetual" and "perpetuity." Entered English in the early 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Media representations can **perpetuate** harmful stereotypes if they consistently portray certain groups in a limited range of roles." — *The Guardian*
2. "The cycle of poverty is **perpetuated** when children from disadvantaged backgrounds lack access to quality education." — *The Economist*
3. "Some historians argue that monuments to controversial figures **perpetuate** a distorted version of history." — *Smithsonian Magazine*
4. "The organism has evolved mechanisms to **perpetuate** its genetic material across generations." — *Nature*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** maintain, sustain, preserve, continue, prolong, keep alive, carry on, extend, immortalize
**Antonyms:** end, terminate, halt, stop, eradicate, abolish, destroy, discontinue, eliminate

## Collocations
- perpetuate a **myth** / **stereotype** / **misconception**
- perpetuate **inequality** / **injustice** / **discrimination**
- perpetuate the **cycle** / **pattern** / **tradition**
- **help** / **tend to** / **serve to** perpetuate
- **systems** / **structures** that perpetuate

## TOEFL Tips
- Commonly appears in **sociology, history, and cultural studies** passages on the TOEFL, often in discussions about how social structures, beliefs, or inequalities are maintained over time.
- The word frequently carries a **negative connotation** in TOEFL passages, used to describe the continuation of something undesirable (stereotypes, inequality, myths).
- Understanding "perpetuate" is important for **rhetorical purpose** questions, where you may be asked why an author mentions certain factors that "perpetuate" a particular condition.
- The related adjective "perpetual" (meaning "never-ending") also appears on the TOEFL and is worth learning together.
