# Unprecedented

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ʌnˈpres.ɪ.den.tɪd/
- Simplified: un-PRES-ih-den-tid

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Never done or known before; without previous example.
2. Having no precedent; novel or unparalleled.

## Etymology
From *un-* ("not") + *precedented* ("having a precedent"), from *precedent*, from Latin *praecedens* ("going before"), present participle of *praecedere* ("to go before"), from *prae-* ("before") + *cedere* ("to go"). Entered English in the early 17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The pandemic caused unprecedented disruption to global supply chains and economic activity." -- *The Economist*
2. "The court's ruling was unprecedented in its scope, effectively rewriting decades of established legal doctrine." -- *The New York Times*
3. "Scientists are observing unprecedented rates of ice loss in the Arctic, far exceeding previous projections." -- *Nature*
4. "The unprecedented scale of the humanitarian crisis demanded a coordinated international response." -- *The Guardian*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** unparalleled, unmatched, unrivaled, extraordinary, exceptional, novel, groundbreaking
**Antonyms:** precedented, common, usual, ordinary, typical, familiar

## Word Family
- **Noun:** precedent (the root word, meaning an earlier occurrence used as an example)
- **Adjective:** unprecedented, precedented (rare)

## Collocations
- unprecedented scale/level
- unprecedented move/step
- unprecedented growth/change
- historically unprecedented
- unprecedented in (history/scope)

## TOEFL Tips
- One of the most frequently used words in academic and journalistic writing, especially in discussions of recent events.
- Understanding the root word "precedent" (a prior example that serves as a guide) is key to understanding this word.
- Commonly appears in reading passages about historical turning points, scientific breakthroughs, and social change.
- A powerful word for the independent writing task when arguing that current events are unique or transformative.
