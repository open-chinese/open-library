# Controversy

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ňąk…íntr…ôv…úňźrsi/ (US: /ňąk…Ďňźntr…ôv…úňźrsi/)
- **Simplified:** KON-truh-vur-see

## Definition
**Noun**
1. Prolonged public disagreement or heated debate, typically about a matter of opinion or policy.
2. A dispute or argument, especially one that is widespread and involves strong opposing views.

## Etymology
From Latin *controversia* ("a turning against, a dispute"), from *controversus* ("turned against, disputed"), from *contra-* ("against") + *versus* (past participle of *vertere*, "to turn"). Entered English in the 14th century through Old French. Related to "controvert" and "incontrovertible."

## Usage Examples
1. "The proposed construction of the dam has generated considerable controversy among environmentalists and local communities." -- *The Guardian*
2. "The scientific controversy surrounding climate change has largely been settled within the research community, though public debate continues."
3. "Her appointment to the Supreme Court was surrounded by controversy due to her lack of judicial experience."
4. "The controversy over standardized testing in schools has persisted for decades without clear resolution."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** debate, dispute, disagreement, contention, conflict, argument, polemic, furor
**Antonyms:** agreement, consensus, accord, harmony, unanimity

- controversy arises / erupts
- avoid / settle a controversy

## TOEFL Tips
- One of the most frequently appearing nouns in TOEFL reading and listening sections.
- Often used in passages about social issues, scientific debates, and historical events.
- The adjective form "controversial" is equally important and appears even more frequently.
- Note the pronunciation: stress falls on the first syllable in American English (KON-truh-vur-see), not the second.
- In TOEFL writing, you may use this word when discussing debatable topics in independent essays.
