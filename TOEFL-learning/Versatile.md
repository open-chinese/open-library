# Versatile

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈvɜːr.sə.tɪl/ (American) or /ˈvɜː.sə.taɪl/ (British)
- **Simplified:** VUR-suh-til (American) or VUR-suh-tyle (British)

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Able to adapt or be adapted to many different functions, activities, or situations; having many uses or applications.
2. (Of a person) Having a wide range of skills, talents, or abilities; capable of turning easily from one thing to another.
3. (Biology) Capable of moving or turning freely in all directions, as an antenna or toe.

## Etymology
From French *versatile* or directly from Latin *versatilis* ("turning, revolving, capable of turning to varied subjects"), from *versare* ("to turn, change"), frequentative of *vertere* ("to turn"). Related to "verse," "version," "reverse," and "convert." Entered English in the early 17th century.

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** adaptable, flexible, multifaceted, all-around, multipurpose, resourceful, multitalented, all-purpose
**Antonyms:** limited, inflexible, specialized, narrow, one-dimensional, rigid, restricted

## TOEFL Tips
- Appears in **reading passages** about biology (organisms with versatile adaptations), technology (versatile tools or materials), and the arts (versatile performers or art forms).
- In vocabulary-in-context questions, "versatile" is commonly paired with "adaptable" or "flexible" as correct synonyms.
- The noun "versatility" is equally important and frequently appears in academic discussions about the advantages of a material, organism, or approach.
- Note the pronunciation difference between American English (/ˈvɜːr.sə.tɪl/) and British English (/ˈvɜː.sə.taɪl/). The TOEFL listening section uses American English.
- In the **writing section**, "versatile" is a useful adjective for describing tools, methods, or solutions that have multiple applications or benefits.
