# Scrutinize

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˈskruː.tɪ.naɪz/
- Simplified: SKROO-tih-nyz
- Also spelled: scrutinise (British English)

## Definition
**Verb**
1. To examine or inspect closely and thoroughly.
2. To look at critically or searchingly; to analyze carefully.

## Etymology
From Latin *scrutinium* ("a search, inquiry"), from *scrutari* ("to search, examine thoroughly"), originally "to sort through trash," from *scruta* ("rags, trash"). Entered English in the early 17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Lawmakers are scrutinizing the proposed legislation for potential loopholes that could benefit special interest groups." -- *The New York Times*
2. "The company's accounting practices were scrutinized by federal regulators following allegations of fraud." -- *Financial Times*
3. "Scientists scrutinized the data for months before publishing their controversial findings." -- *Nature*
4. "Every aspect of the candidate's personal and professional life was scrutinized by the media during the campaign." -- *The Washington Post*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** examine, inspect, analyze, investigate, study, review, probe, survey
**Antonyms:** ignore, overlook, neglect, skim, glance at, disregard

## Word Family
- **Noun:** scrutiny
- **Adjective:** scrutinizing

## Collocations
- scrutinize closely/carefully
- scrutinize the data/evidence
- subject to scrutiny
- under scrutiny
- public/media scrutiny

## TOEFL Tips
- The noun "scrutiny" is more common than the verb form in academic writing and frequently appears on the TOEFL.
- "Under scrutiny" and "subject to scrutiny" are important fixed expressions to recognize.
- Often appears in passages about scientific peer review, government oversight, or investigative journalism.
- Understanding this word helps with questions about how evidence or claims are evaluated in academic contexts.
