# Obscure

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /əbˈskjʊər/
- **Simplified:** uhb-SKYOOR

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Not discovered or known about; uncertain or unclear.
2. Not well known; little recognized by the general public.
3. Difficult to understand or make sense of; not clearly expressed.
4. Hard to see or distinguish; dim or indistinct.

**Verb (transitive)**
1. To keep from being seen; to conceal or hide.
2. To make unclear, unintelligible, or difficult to understand.
3. To overshadow; to make less prominent or important.

## Etymology
From Latin *obscurus* ("dark, dim, unclear"), possibly from *ob-* ("over") + a root related to *scurus* ("covered"). Entered English in the 14th century through Old French *obscur*. The same root gives us "obscurity" and "obscurantism." Related to the idea of something being hidden in darkness or shadow.

## Usage Examples
1. "The origins of the tradition remain obscure, with scholars offering competing theories about how it began." -- *Smithsonian Magazine*
2. "She specializes in translating obscure medieval texts that have received little scholarly attention."
3. "Heavy fog obscured the runway, forcing the airport to delay all departures."
4. "The use of technical jargon can obscure the meaning of scientific findings for the general public."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** (adjective) unclear, vague, ambiguous, unknown, little-known, hidden, abstruse, arcane, esoteric; (verb) conceal, hide, cloud, mask, shroud, eclipse
**Antonyms:** (adjective) clear, obvious, well-known, famous, prominent, evident, apparent; (verb) reveal, clarify, illuminate, expose

## TOEFL Tips
- A versatile word that appears frequently in both reading and listening sections across many academic disciplines.
- As an adjective, it often describes texts, origins, or figures that are not well known -- common in history and literature passages.
- As a verb, it appears in science and social science passages where one factor hides or masks another.
- Pay attention to whether "obscure" is used as an adjective (describing something unclear/unknown) or a verb (making something unclear).
- In TOEFL writing, "obscure" is useful for discussing clarity: "Excessive jargon obscures rather than illuminates the argument."
- The phrase "rise from obscurity" commonly appears in biographical passages about figures who became famous after being unknown.
