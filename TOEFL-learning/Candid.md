# Candid

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈkændɪd/
- **Simplified:** KAN-did

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Truthful and straightforward; frank and open in expression.
2. (Of a photograph) taken informally, especially without the subject's knowledge or preparation.
3. Impartial and free from bias or prejudice.

## Etymology
From Latin *candidus* ("white, bright, shining"), from *candere* ("to shine, to glow"). The connection between "white/bright" and "honest" comes from the Roman tradition where candidates for office wore white togas (*toga candida*) to symbolize purity and honesty. Entered English in the 17th century through French *candide*.

## Usage Examples
1. "The senator gave a remarkably candid interview in which she acknowledged the failures of her administration." -- *The Washington Post*
2. "His candid assessment of the company's financial situation alarmed many of the shareholders."
3. "The photographer was known for her candid portraits that captured people in unguarded moments."
4. "To be candid, I do not believe this approach will yield the results we are hoping for."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** frank, honest, forthright, straightforward, open, direct, blunt, outspoken, sincere
**Antonyms:** secretive, evasive, dishonest, guarded, deceptive, insincere, disingenuous

## Collocations
- candid + assessment / opinion / view
- candid + conversation / discussion / interview
- candid + photograph / photo / shot
- brutally / refreshingly / remarkably candid
- to be candid (about something)

## TOEFL Tips
- Often appears in reading passages about interviews, personal accounts, or direct communication styles.
- Commonly tested in vocabulary questions where it can be replaced by "frank" or "honest."
- Do not confuse "candid" with "candidate," although they share the same Latin root.
- In TOEFL listening, speakers may use "to be candid" as a discourse marker to signal they are about to share an honest, possibly uncomfortable opinion.
