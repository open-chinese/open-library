# Inherent

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ɪnˈhɪə.rənt/ or /ɪnˈher.ənt/
- Simplified: in-HEER-uhnt or in-HAIR-uhnt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Existing in something as a permanent, essential, or characteristic attribute; intrinsic.
2. Vested in someone as a right or privilege.
3. Naturally and inseparably a part of something.

## Etymology
From Latin *inhaerens*, present participle of *inhaerere* ("to stick in, cling to, adhere"), from *in-* ("in") + *haerere* ("to stick"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "There are inherent risks in any surgical procedure, but the benefits in this case far outweigh them." -- *The Lancet*
2. "Critics argue that the inherent bias in the algorithm perpetuates existing social inequalities." -- *The New York Times*
3. "The inherent complexity of the climate system makes long-term predictions extremely difficult." -- *Nature*
4. "Freedom of speech is often regarded as an inherent right in democratic societies." -- *The Economist*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** intrinsic, innate, built-in, fundamental, essential, natural, inborn
**Antonyms:** extrinsic, acquired, external, superficial, incidental, adventitious

## Word Family
- **Adverb:** inherently
- **Verb:** inhere (formal/rare)

## Collocations
- inherent in (something)
- inherent risk/danger
- inherent bias/flaw
- inherent limitation/weakness
- inherent quality/characteristic

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently used in academic passages to describe fundamental qualities or unavoidable characteristics.
- "Inherently" (adverb) is very common in lectures and academic writing.
- Often confused with "inherited" -- "inherent" means built-in or intrinsic, while "inherited" means received from a predecessor.
- Appears in science, philosophy, and social science passages on the TOEFL.
