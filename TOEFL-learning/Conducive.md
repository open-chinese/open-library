# Conducive

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /kənˈdjuːsɪv/
- **Simplified:** kuhn-DOO-siv

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Making a certain situation or outcome likely or possible; tending to promote or assist.
2. Contributing to or favorable to a particular result.

*Note:* Almost always followed by the preposition "to."

## Etymology
From Latin *conducere* ("to lead together, to bring together"), from *con-* ("together") + *ducere* ("to lead"). The adjective form developed in the 17th century from the earlier English verb "conduce" (meaning "to lead toward a result"). Related to "conduct," "deduce," and "produce."

## Usage Examples
1. "The quiet atmosphere of the library is conducive to focused study and deep concentration."
2. "Research suggests that a moderate level of stress can actually be conducive to improved performance." -- *Psychology Today*
3. "The warm, humid climate of the region is particularly conducive to the growth of tropical plants."
4. "Creating an environment conducive to innovation requires both freedom and accountability."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** favorable, helpful, beneficial, advantageous, supportive, contributory, promoting
**Antonyms:** detrimental, harmful, unfavorable, hindering, obstructive, counterproductive

## Collocations
- conducive to + learning / growth / development
- conducive to + health / well-being / success
- conducive + environment / atmosphere / conditions
- highly / particularly / especially conducive
- not conducive to / hardly conducive to

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently appears in reading passages about education, work environments, and scientific conditions.
- Almost always used with the preposition "to" -- remember the pattern "conducive to + noun/gerund."
- Common in TOEFL writing prompts where you describe favorable conditions (e.g., "a classroom environment conducive to learning").
- May appear in listening lectures about factors that promote or hinder certain outcomes.
