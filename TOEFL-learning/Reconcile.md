# Reconcile

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈrɛk.ən.saɪl/
- **Simplified:** REK-un-syle

## Definition
**Verb**
1. **(transitive)** To restore friendly relations between; to cause people or groups to become amicable again after a conflict.
2. **(transitive)** To make two apparently conflicting things compatible or consistent with each other.
3. **(transitive)** To settle or resolve a disagreement, dispute, or difference.
4. **(reflexive)** To accept something unpleasant or unwelcome. *She reconciled herself to the loss.*
5. **(transitive, finance)** To check the consistency of financial accounts; to make accounts agree.

## Etymology
From Latin *reconciliare* ("to bring together again, reunite, win over again"), from *re-* ("again") + *conciliare* ("to bring together, unite"), from *concilium* ("council, assembly"). Entered English in the 14th century through Old French.

## Usage Examples
1. "Scientists have struggled to **reconcile** the predictions of quantum mechanics with those of general relativity." — *Scientific American*
2. "The peace agreement sought to **reconcile** the two communities that had been divided by decades of conflict." — *The Guardian*
3. "The historian attempted to **reconcile** the contradictory accounts given by different eyewitnesses." — *The Journal of American History*
4. "Individuals must often **reconcile** their personal ambitions with their responsibilities to family and community."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** harmonize, resolve, settle, reunite, make compatible, square, balance, accommodate, bring into agreement
**Antonyms:** estrange, alienate, separate, conflict, divide, oppose, clash

## TOEFL Tips
- Appears in **reading and listening** sections across multiple disciplines, including science (reconciling conflicting theories), history (reconciling different accounts), and social studies (reconciling opposing perspectives).
- The noun "reconciliation" is equally important and often appears in passages about post-conflict societies, peace processes, or scientific progress.
- On the TOEFL, "reconcile" frequently appears when an author or lecturer discusses how to make sense of two seemingly contradictory pieces of evidence or viewpoints.
- In **rhetorical purpose** questions, understanding that an author is attempting to "reconcile" two positions helps you identify the organizational structure of a passage.
- The adjective "irreconcilable" (meaning impossible to make compatible) also appears on the TOEFL.
