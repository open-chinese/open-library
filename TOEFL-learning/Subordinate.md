# Subordinate

## Pronunciation
- **Adjective/Noun:** IPA: /səˈbɔː.dɪ.nət/ -- Simplified: suh-BOR-dih-nuht
- **Verb:** IPA: /səˈbɔː.dɪ.neɪt/ -- Simplified: suh-BOR-dih-nayt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Lower in rank or position; secondary in importance.
2. (Grammar) Denoting a clause that cannot stand alone as a sentence (e.g., a clause introduced by "because," "although," or "when").

**Noun**
3. A person under the authority or control of another within an organization.

**Verb**
4. To treat or regard as less important than something else; to make secondary.
5. To place in a lower position of rank or importance.

## Etymology
From Medieval Latin *subordinatus*, past participle of *subordinare* ("to place in a lower order"), from Latin *sub-* ("under") + *ordinare* ("to order, arrange"), from *ordo* ("order, rank"). Entered English in the mid-15th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The manager was criticized for failing to delegate responsibilities to her subordinates." -- *Harvard Business Review*
2. "Individual interests must sometimes be subordinated to the collective good in times of crisis." -- *The Economist*
3. "The treaty effectively subordinated national sovereignty to international law on environmental matters." -- *The New York Times*
4. "In complex sentences, subordinate clauses provide additional information that depends on the main clause."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** (adj.) secondary, inferior, lower, minor, lesser, ancillary; (noun) underling, junior, assistant
**Antonyms:** (adj.) superior, dominant, primary, chief, principal; (noun) superior, boss, leader

## Word Family
- **Noun:** subordinate, subordination
- **Verb:** subordinate
- **Adjective:** subordinate

## Collocations

- subordinate clause (grammar)
- subordinate to (something)
- direct subordinate

## TOEFL Tips
- Appears in both content-area passages and grammar-related contexts on the TOEFL.
- Understanding "subordinate clause" is essential for the structure of academic writing.
- The verb form (to subordinate) often appears in social science and history passages about power dynamics.
- Note the pronunciation difference between the adjective/noun and verb forms -- this is a common pattern in English that the TOEFL tests.
