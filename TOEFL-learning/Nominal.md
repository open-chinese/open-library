# Nominal

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈnɒmɪnəl/
- **Simplified:** NOM-ih-nuhl

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Existing in name only; not real or actual despite having a particular title or designation.
2. Very small; token or insignificant (especially of a price, fee, or amount).
3. (Grammar) Relating to or functioning as a noun.
4. (Economics) Measured in terms of current prices or face value, without adjusting for inflation (opposite of "real").

## Etymology
From Latin *nominalis* ("of or pertaining to a name"), from *nomen* ("name"). Entered English in the 15th century. The semantic development from "in name" to "very small" reflects the idea that something exists as a formality (in name) but has little real substance. Related to "nominate," "nomenclature," "noun," and "denomination."

## Usage Examples
1. "The king served as the nominal head of state, while real political power rested with the prime minister." -- *The Economist*
2. "The organization charges a nominal fee of one dollar for membership to cover administrative costs."
3. "Nominal GDP growth of 5% may actually represent a decline in real terms once inflation is taken into account."
4. "Although he held the nominal title of director, he had no authority over daily operations."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** (in-name-only sense) titular, formal, symbolic, token, ostensible, theoretical; (small-amount sense) minimal, token, trivial, insignificant, negligible
**Antonyms:** (in-name-only sense) real, actual, genuine, true, substantive; (small-amount sense) substantial, considerable, significant

## TOEFL Tips
- Appears frequently in economics and political science passages.
- Understanding the "in name only" vs. "very small" meanings is critical for correct interpretation in context.
- The economics usage (nominal vs. real) is particularly important for TOEFL: "nominal" values are unadjusted for inflation, while "real" values account for it.
- In vocabulary questions, look at context carefully to determine which sense is intended.
- May appear in history passages describing figurehead rulers who held nominal power while others governed.
