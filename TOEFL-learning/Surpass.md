# Surpass

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /sərˈpæs/
- **Simplified:** sur-PASS

## Definition
**Verb**
1. To exceed or go beyond in degree, amount, or quality; to be greater than
2. To be better than; to outdo or excel

## Etymology
From French *surpasser*, from *sur-* ("over, above") + *passer* ("to pass"). Entered English in the mid-16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Solar energy production is expected to surpass coal-generated electricity within the next decade." — *International Energy Agency*
2. "The student's performance on the final exam surpassed all expectations." — *Journal of Educational Psychology*
3. "China's economic output has surpassed that of many industrialized nations in recent years." — *The Economist*
4. "The new telescope's imaging capabilities surpass those of any previous instrument." — *NASA Research Reports*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** exceed, outdo, outstrip, transcend, eclipse, top, excel, outperform
**Antonyms:** fall behind, trail, lag, fail, underperform, fall short of

## Word Family
- **Surpassing** (adjective): exceptional; greatly exceeding others
- **Surpassingly** (adverb): to a surpassing degree
- **Unsurpassed** (adjective): not exceeded; supreme

## Collocations
- surpass expectations
- surpass a record
- surpass a goal
- surpass a threshold
- surpass in quality
- far surpass

## TOEFL Tips
"Surpass" is a commonly tested word in TOEFL reading passages, particularly in contexts involving comparisons, achievements, and growth trends. It frequently appears in economics, science, and history passages. When answering vocabulary-in-context questions, "exceed" is the most reliable synonym. The word implies going beyond a benchmark or standard, not merely reaching it. In the TOEFL writing and speaking sections, "surpass" is an effective word for expressing the idea that one thing has gone beyond another in quality or quantity.
