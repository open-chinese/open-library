# Demographic

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˌdɛməˈɡræfɪk/
- **Simplified:** dem-uh-GRAF-ik

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Relating to the structure or composition of populations, especially in terms of age, race, gender, income, education, and other statistical characteristics.

**Noun**
1. A particular sector or segment of a population, defined by shared characteristics.
2. (plural: demographics) Statistical data relating to the population and the groups within it.

## Etymology
From Greek *demos* ("people") + *graphein* ("to write, to describe"). The word "demography" (the study of populations) was coined in the 19th century by French statistician Achille Guillard. The adjective/noun form "demographic" followed shortly after. Related words sharing the *demos* root include "democracy," "epidemic," and "pandemic."

## Usage Examples
1. "The demographic shift toward an aging population poses significant challenges for social security systems worldwide." -- *The Economist*
2. "Advertisers carefully analyze the demographics of television audiences to target their campaigns effectively."
3. "The study examined demographic factors such as income level, education, and ethnicity to identify health disparities."
4. "Millennials have become the largest demographic group in the workforce, surpassing baby boomers."

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently appears in reading passages about sociology, urban studies, economics, and public policy.
- Understanding this word is essential for passages about population changes, migration patterns, and social trends.
- The noun form "demographics" (plural) is particularly common in business and marketing contexts.
- In TOEFL listening, professors may discuss "demographic trends" or "demographic shifts" when explaining societal changes.
- Often appears alongside words like "census," "population," "migration," and "urbanization."
