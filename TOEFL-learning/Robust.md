# Robust

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /roʊˈbʌst/
- **Simplified:** roh-BUST

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Strong and healthy; vigorous
2. Sturdy in construction; able to withstand demanding conditions
3. (Of a system, process, or argument) Strong and effective in all or most situations; not easily disrupted
4. Rich and full-bodied in flavor or character

## Etymology
From Latin *robustus* ("strong, firm, solid, oaken"), from *robur* ("strength, hard timber, oak"). Entered English in the mid-16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The researchers developed a robust methodology that produced consistent results across multiple trials." — *Nature Methods*
2. "A robust economy depends on diverse industries and strong consumer confidence." — *The Wall Street Journal*
3. "The new software features a robust security framework designed to withstand sophisticated cyberattacks." — *MIT Technology Review*
4. "Despite harsh weather conditions, the species maintains a robust population in the northern territories." — *Ecology Letters*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** strong, sturdy, vigorous, resilient, durable, hardy, powerful, sound
**Antonyms:** weak, fragile, frail, delicate, feeble, flimsy, vulnerable

## Word Family
- **Robustly** (adverb): in a robust manner
- **Robustness** (noun): the quality of being robust

## Collocations
- robust economy
- robust growth
- robust system
- robust evidence
- robust debate

- robust framework

## TOEFL Tips
"Robust" is a versatile word that appears frequently in TOEFL passages across multiple disciplines, including economics, science, and technology. In academic contexts, it often describes research methods ("robust analysis"), economic conditions ("robust growth"), or system design ("robust infrastructure"). When tested as a vocabulary question, common correct answers include "strong," "vigorous," or "resilient." Pay attention to context, as "robust" in a scientific passage typically emphasizes reliability and consistency rather than mere physical strength.
