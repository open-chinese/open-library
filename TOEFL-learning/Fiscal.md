# Fiscal

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈfɪskəl/
- **Simplified:** FIS-kuhl

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Relating to government revenue, especially taxes and public spending.
2. Relating to financial matters in general, particularly those of a government or public body.

**Noun**
1. (In some countries) A public prosecutor or legal officer.

## Etymology
From Latin *fiscalis* ("of or belonging to the treasury"), from *fiscus* ("basket, purse, public treasury"). In ancient Rome, the *fiscus* was originally a basket used for collecting taxes, which later came to refer to the imperial treasury itself. Entered English in the 16th century through French *fiscal*.

## Usage Examples
1. "The government announced a new fiscal policy aimed at stimulating economic growth through targeted tax cuts." -- *The Financial Times*
2. "The fiscal year for most U.S. federal agencies begins on October 1 and ends on September 30."
3. "Critics argue that the country's fiscal irresponsibility has led to unsustainable levels of national debt."
4. "Fiscal austerity measures, including spending cuts and tax increases, were imposed as conditions for the international bailout."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** financial, budgetary, economic, monetary, pecuniary, treasury-related
**Antonyms:** (No direct antonym; "monetary" is sometimes contrasted with "fiscal" in economics)

## TOEFL Tips
- Extremely common in economics and political science reading passages.
- Important to distinguish "fiscal" (related to government taxing and spending) from "monetary" (related to money supply and interest rates, usually controlled by a central bank).
- The term "fiscal year" appears frequently in business and government contexts on the TOEFL.
- Often paired with "policy" -- understanding "fiscal policy" vs. "monetary policy" is valuable for TOEFL economics passages.
- In TOEFL listening lectures on economics, professors frequently discuss fiscal measures taken by governments.
