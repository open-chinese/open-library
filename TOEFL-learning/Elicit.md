# Elicit

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ɪˈlɪs.ɪt/
- **Simplified:** ih-LIS-it

## Definition
**Verb**
1. To draw out or bring forth (a response, answer, or fact) from someone
2. To evoke or provoke (a reaction or emotion)
3. To succeed in obtaining information or a reaction from someone, especially with effort or skill

## Etymology
From Latin *elicitus*, past participle of *elicere* ("to draw out, entice"), from *e-/ex-* ("out") + *lacere* ("to lure, deceive"). Entered English in the mid-17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The interviewer's carefully worded questions were designed to elicit honest responses from the participants." — *Journal of Applied Psychology*
2. "The documentary elicited strong emotional reactions from audiences around the world." — *The Guardian*
3. "The new treatment elicited a significant immune response in the majority of trial participants." — *The New England Journal of Medicine*
4. "Teachers use open-ended questions to elicit critical thinking and deeper engagement from students." — *Educational Research Review*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** evoke, draw out, extract, provoke, prompt, obtain, bring forth, induce
**Antonyms:** suppress, repress, stifle, quell, inhibit, withhold

## Word Family
- **Elicitation** (noun): the act of eliciting
- **Elicited** (past tense/past participle): drawn out or evoked
- **Eliciting** (present participle): in the process of drawing out

## TOEFL Tips
"Elicit" is commonly tested on the TOEFL, and a critical point is to not confuse it with "illicit" (meaning illegal or unlawful). Despite their similar pronunciation, they are entirely different words. "Elicit" is a verb meaning "to draw out," while "illicit" is an adjective meaning "forbidden by law." In reading passages, "elicit" frequently appears in research methodology contexts (eliciting responses from subjects) and in discussions of how events provoke reactions. When tested as a vocabulary question, "draw out" or "evoke" are typical correct answers.
