# Disseminate

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /dɪˈsɛmɪneɪt/
- **Simplified:** dih-SEM-ih-nayt

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To spread or disperse information, knowledge, or ideas widely.
2. To scatter or distribute broadly, as seeds are scattered for planting.

## Etymology
From Latin *disseminatus*, past participle of *disseminare* ("to scatter seed, to spread abroad"), from *dis-* ("in every direction") + *seminare* ("to sow, to plant"), from *semen* ("seed"). Entered English in the early 17th century. The metaphor of "sowing seeds" captures the idea of spreading information so it takes root in many places.

## Usage Examples
1. "The World Health Organization works to disseminate critical health information to communities in remote areas." -- *The Lancet*
2. "Social media platforms have dramatically accelerated the speed at which news and misinformation are disseminated."
3. "The researchers disseminated their findings through peer-reviewed journals and international conferences."
4. "The government established a task force to disseminate accurate information about the new vaccination program."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** spread, distribute, circulate, broadcast, propagate, disperse, publicize, promulgate
**Antonyms:** conceal, suppress, withhold, hide, collect, gather, hoard

## Collocations

## TOEFL Tips
- Highly common in academic reading passages, especially those about research, education, and public health.
- Often appears in the context of how scientific findings or public information reach broader audiences.
- Tested as a synonym for "spread" or "distribute" in vocabulary-in-context questions.
- In TOEFL writing, this is an excellent academic-register word to use when discussing how ideas or information spread.
- Distinguished from "distribute" by its stronger emphasis on widespread, broad dispersal rather than organized allocation.
