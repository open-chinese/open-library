# Threshold

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈθrɛʃ.hoʊld/ (American) or /ˈθrɛʃ.əʊld/ (British)
- **Simplified:** THRESH-hold

## Definition
**Noun (countable)**
1. The level or point at which something begins to take effect or becomes true; a critical value that must be exceeded for a certain result to occur.
2. The strip of wood, metal, or stone forming the bottom of a doorway; a doorsill.
3. The beginning or starting point of an experience, event, or venture. *On the threshold of a new era.*
4. (Physiology/Psychology) The minimum level of stimulation required to produce a response or sensation.

## Etymology
From Old English *therscold*, *threscold* ("doorsill"), from *threscan* ("to thresh, trample") + an uncertain second element possibly related to Old Norse *threskjoldr*. The original sense was a piece of wood or stone at the foot of a door. The figurative sense ("point of beginning" or "critical level") developed in the 19th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "If global temperatures exceed the **threshold** of 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, the consequences could be catastrophic." — *IPCC Report*
2. "The pain **threshold** varies significantly from person to person, influenced by both genetics and psychology." — *The Lancet*
3. "Income must fall below a certain **threshold** for families to qualify for government assistance programs." — *The Economist*
4. "The country stood on the **threshold** of independence after decades of colonial rule." — *The Guardian*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** limit, boundary, point, level, brink, verge, doorstep, starting point, minimum, cutoff
**Antonyms:** maximum, ceiling, center, middle, interior

## TOEFL Tips
- Widely used in **science, economics, and psychology** passages on the TOEFL. In science, it refers to the minimum level needed to trigger a reaction or effect. In economics, it refers to income or numerical boundaries.
- The figurative meaning ("on the threshold of") is common in history and social science passages, meaning "at the beginning of" or "about to enter."
- Understanding "threshold" is important for passages about **climate change** (temperature thresholds), **biology** (stimulus thresholds), and **public policy** (eligibility thresholds).
- In vocabulary-in-context questions, be prepared to identify whether "threshold" is being used literally (doorway) or figuratively (critical point), as the TOEFL often tests this distinction.
