# Supplement

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈsʌp.lɪ.mənt/ (noun), /ˈsʌp.lɪ.mɛnt/ (verb)
- **Simplified:** SUP-luh-ment (noun), SUP-luh-ment (verb)

## Definition
**Noun**
1. Something added to complete a thing, make up for a deficiency, or extend or strengthen the whole
2. A section added to a book, document, or periodical to provide further information
3. A dietary product taken to add nutritional value to the diet

**Verb**
1. To add something extra to; to provide an addition to something
2. To make up for a deficiency in; to enhance or complete

## Etymology
From Latin *supplementum* ("a filling up, supply"), from *supplere* ("to fill up, complete"), from *sub-* ("from below, up") + *plere* ("to fill"). Entered English in the 14th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Many students supplement their classroom learning with online courses and tutorials." — *The Chronicle of Higher Education*
2. "The government introduced a program to supplement the income of low-wage workers." — *The Economist*
3. "The textbook includes a digital supplement with interactive exercises and additional readings." — *Cambridge University Press*
4. "Research suggests that vitamin D supplements may benefit individuals living in northern latitudes with limited sun exposure." — *The New England Journal of Medicine*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** addition, complement, enhancement, appendix, addendum, augmentation, extra
**Antonyms:** reduction, deduction, decrease, subtraction, core, essential

## Word Family
- **Supplemental** (adjective): provided in addition; extra
- **Supplementary** (adjective): serving to supplement; additional
- **Supplementation** (noun): the act of supplementing

## Collocations

- supplement income

## TOEFL Tips
"Supplement" appears in TOEFL passages across many disciplines, from nutrition science to education and economics. It functions as both a noun and a verb, so pay attention to its role in the sentence. In reading passages, it often appears in contexts about enhancing or adding to something that already exists. A common TOEFL trap is confusing "supplement" with "complement" -- a supplement adds to something that is deficient, while a complement completes something by fitting together with it. Both noun and verb forms are stressed on the first syllable.
