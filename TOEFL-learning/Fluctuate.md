# Fluctuate

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˈflʌk.tʃu.eɪt/
- Simplified: FLUK-choo-ayt

## Definition
**Verb**
1. To rise and fall irregularly in number or amount; to vary unpredictably.
2. To shift back and forth uncertainly; to waver.

## Etymology
From Latin *fluctuatus*, past participle of *fluctuare* ("to wave, undulate"), from *fluctus* ("a wave, flowing"), from *fluere* ("to flow"). Entered English in the mid-17th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "Oil prices fluctuated wildly throughout the quarter, driven by geopolitical uncertainty and shifting demand." -- *Financial Times*
2. "Temperatures in the region can fluctuate by as much as 30 degrees within a single day." -- *National Geographic*
3. "The exchange rate has fluctuated significantly since the central bank abandoned its fixed-rate policy." -- *The Economist*
4. "Public opinion on the issue has fluctuated over the past decade, reflecting broader cultural shifts."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** vary, oscillate, waver, vacillate, swing, shift, alternate
**Antonyms:** stabilize, remain constant, steady, persist, hold

## Word Family
- **Noun:** fluctuation (plural: fluctuations)
- **Adjective:** fluctuating

## Collocations
- fluctuate widely/significantly
- fluctuate between (two values)
- prices/rates fluctuate
- fluctuate over time
- temperature/demand fluctuates

## TOEFL Tips
- Commonly appears in reading and listening passages about economics, climate science, and natural phenomena.
- The noun form "fluctuation" is equally important and frequently tested.
- Often appears in graphs and charts described in integrated writing and speaking tasks.
- Understanding this word helps with detail and inference questions about trends described in passages.
