# Paradox

## Pronunciation
- IPA: /ˈpær.ə.dɒks/
- Simplified: PAIR-uh-doks

## Definition
**Noun**
1. A seemingly absurd or self-contradictory statement or proposition that when investigated or explained may prove to be well-founded or true.
2. A situation, person, or thing that combines contradictory features or qualities.
3. (Logic) A statement that contradicts itself, such as "This statement is false."

## Etymology
From Latin *paradoxum*, from Greek *paradoxon* ("contrary to expectation"), from *para-* ("contrary to") + *doxa* ("opinion"), from *dokein* ("to think, suppose"). Entered English in the mid-16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The paradox of thrift -- that increased saving by individuals can lead to decreased overall economic growth -- remains a central concept in macroeconomics." -- *The Economist*
2. "It is a paradox of modern life that we are more connected than ever yet report feeling increasingly isolated." -- *The New York Times*
3. "The French paradox -- low rates of heart disease despite a diet rich in saturated fats -- has intrigued researchers for decades." -- *The Lancet*
4. "The study revealed a curious paradox: students who spent more time studying sometimes performed worse on standardized tests."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** contradiction, inconsistency, anomaly, enigma, puzzle, incongruity
**Antonyms:** certainty, truism, consistency, axiom

## Word Family
- **Adjective:** paradoxical
- **Adverb:** paradoxically

## Collocations
- apparent paradox
- paradox of (something)
- resolve a paradox
- central/fundamental paradox
- curious/strange paradox

## TOEFL Tips
- Very common in reading passages, especially those dealing with science, philosophy, and social sciences.
- TOEFL lectures often introduce a paradox to frame a discussion or research question.
- "Paradoxically" is frequently used in academic texts and is worth memorizing as a transition word.
- Understanding paradoxes is essential for inference and rhetorical purpose questions on the TOEFL.
