# Complement

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈkɒm.plɪ.mənt/
- **Simplified:** KOM-pluh-ment

## Definition
**Noun**
1. A thing that completes or brings to perfection; something that fills up or makes whole
2. The number or quantity needed to make a group complete
3. (Grammar) A word or phrase used to complete a grammatical construction
4. (Mathematics) The amount by which a given angle is less than 90 degrees

**Verb**
1. To add to something in a way that enhances or improves it; to make complete or perfect

## Etymology
From Latin *complementum* ("that which fills up or completes"), from *complere* ("to fill up"), from *com-* ("together, intensive") + *plere* ("to fill"). Entered English in the late 14th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The new research facility will complement the university's existing laboratories and enhance its scientific capabilities." — *Science*
2. "The red wine served as a perfect complement to the rich flavors of the aged cheese." — *Food & Wine*
3. "Online learning platforms are designed to complement, not replace, traditional classroom instruction." — *The Chronicle of Higher Education*
4. "The ship sailed with its full complement of crew members and passengers." — *Maritime Quarterly*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** counterpart, companion, supplement, completion, addition, enhancement, accompaniment
**Antonyms:** contrast, opposite, contradiction, clash, conflict

## Word Family
- **Complementary** (adjective): combining to form a complete whole; enhancing each other
- **Complementarity** (noun): the state of being complementary
- **Complementation** (noun): the action of complementing

## TOEFL Tips
"Complement" is a frequently tested word on the TOEFL, and it is critical to distinguish it from "compliment" (a word of praise). They sound identical but have entirely different meanings and spellings. "Complement" (with an 'e') means to complete or enhance, while "compliment" (with an 'i') means to praise. In reading passages, "complement" often appears in discussions about how two things work well together. In science passages, look for "complementary" in contexts such as complementary DNA strands or complementary angles. This distinction is a common trap in TOEFL vocabulary questions.
