# Catalyst

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈkæt.əl.ɪst/
- **Simplified:** KAT-uh-list

## Definition
**Noun**
1. (Chemistry) A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself undergoing any permanent chemical change
2. A person or thing that precipitates an event or change; something that causes an important event to happen

## Etymology
From Greek *katalysis* ("dissolution"), from *katalyein* ("to dissolve"), from *kata-* ("down") + *lyein* ("to loosen"). The modern chemical sense was coined in the early 19th century by Swedish chemist Jons Jacob Berzelius. The figurative sense developed in the early 20th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The invention of the printing press served as a catalyst for the spread of knowledge during the Renaissance." — *Smithsonian Magazine*
2. "Enzymes act as biological catalysts, accelerating chemical reactions essential for life." — *Molecular Biology of the Cell*
3. "The economic crisis proved to be the catalyst for sweeping regulatory reforms in the financial sector." — *The Financial Times*
4. "Platinum serves as a catalyst in catalytic converters, helping to reduce harmful vehicle emissions." — *Environmental Science & Technology*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** stimulus, trigger, spark, impetus, spur, driving force, agent of change
**Antonyms:** inhibitor, deterrent, hindrance, impediment, obstacle

## Word Family
- **Catalyze** (verb): to cause or accelerate a reaction or change
- **Catalysis** (noun): the process of catalyzing
- **Catalytic** (adjective): relating to or involving catalysis
- **Catalytically** (adverb): by means of catalysis

## Collocations
- catalyst for change
- catalyst for growth
- act as a catalyst
- serve as a catalyst
- catalytic converter
- catalytic reaction

## TOEFL Tips
"Catalyst" is a dual-context word on the TOEFL, appearing both in science passages (chemistry, biology) and in social science/history passages (as a metaphor for something that triggers change). In chemistry contexts, it refers specifically to a substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed. In broader academic contexts, it refers to anything that causes significant change. Understanding both uses is essential. In reading passages about historical events, "catalyst" is often used to describe the triggering event of a larger movement or transformation.
