# Encompass

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ɪnˈkʌmpəs/
- **Simplified:** in-KUM-puhs

## Definition
**Verb (transitive)**
1. To include or contain comprehensively; to cover a wide range of items, topics, or areas.
2. To surround or encircle completely.
3. To bring about or accomplish (archaic usage).

## Etymology
From *en-* ("in, into") + *compass* ("circle, boundary"), ultimately from Old French *compas* and Latin *compassus*. First used in English in the 16th century, originally meaning "to surround" in a physical sense. The figurative meaning of "to include comprehensively" developed later. Related to "compass" (the navigational instrument, which draws a circle).

## Usage Examples
1. "The new curriculum encompasses a broad range of subjects, from traditional sciences to digital literacy and the arts."
2. "The national park encompasses over two million acres of wilderness, including forests, rivers, and mountain ranges." -- *National Geographic*
3. "Her research encompasses several disciplines, including psychology, neuroscience, and linguistics."
4. "The term 'biodiversity' encompasses the variety of all living organisms at the genetic, species, and ecosystem levels."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** include, comprise, contain, cover, embrace, incorporate, span, involve, surround
**Antonyms:** exclude, omit, neglect, leave out

## Collocations
- encompass + a range / variety / spectrum
- encompass + areas / fields / disciplines
- encompass + aspects / elements / dimensions
- all-encompassing (adjective)
- broadly / widely encompass
- encompass everything from X to Y

## TOEFL Tips
- Frequently appears in academic reading passages when an author describes the scope of a topic, field, or area.
- Often tested as a synonym for "include" or "cover" in vocabulary questions.
- Pay attention to the pattern "encompass everything from X to Y," which is common in TOEFL passages to show breadth.
- Useful in TOEFL writing to describe the scope of a topic: "This issue encompasses economic, social, and environmental dimensions."
- Distinguished from "include" by its emphasis on comprehensiveness -- it implies inclusion of all or most elements, not just some.
