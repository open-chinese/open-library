# Chronic

## Pronunciation
- **IPA:** /ˈkrɒn.ɪk/ (British) or /ˈkrɑː.nɪk/ (American)
- **Simplified:** KRON-ik

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. (Of an illness or condition) Persisting for a long time or constantly recurring; opposite of acute.
2. (Of a problem) Long-lasting and difficult to eradicate or resolve.
3. Having a particular undesirable habit or condition over a prolonged period. *A chronic liar.*
4. (Informal, British) Of very poor quality; dreadful.

## Etymology
From French *chronique*, from Latin *chronicus*, from Greek *khronikos* ("of time"), from *khronos* ("time"). The medical usage, distinguishing long-lasting conditions from acute ones, dates to the late 14th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "**Chronic** diseases such as diabetes and heart disease account for a significant portion of healthcare expenditures worldwide." — *World Health Organization*
2. "The region has suffered from **chronic** water shortages that have intensified in recent decades." — *Scientific American*
3. "**Chronic** exposure to low levels of pollution can cause lasting damage to respiratory health." — *The Lancet*
4. "The country's **chronic** underinvestment in education has contributed to widening inequality."

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms:** persistent, long-lasting, prolonged, enduring, lingering, recurrent, habitual, ongoing
**Antonyms:** acute, temporary, brief, short-lived, fleeting, intermittent, occasional

## TOEFL Tips
- Extremely common in **biology, health science, and environmental science** reading passages on the TOEFL.
- The key distinction tested is between "chronic" (long-lasting) and "acute" (sudden, severe, short-term). Understanding this contrast is essential.
- In TOEFL reading, "chronic" is often used figuratively to describe persistent social or environmental problems, not just medical conditions.
- Pay attention to time-related context clues ("over many years," "persistent," "long-term") that signal the meaning of "chronic."
