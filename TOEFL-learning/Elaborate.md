# Elaborate

## Pronunciation
- **Adjective:** /ɪˈlæb.ər.ət/ — ih-LAB-ur-ut
- **Verb:** /ɪˈlæb.ə.reɪt/ — ih-LAB-uh-rayt

## Definition
**Adjective**
1. Involving many carefully arranged parts or details; intricate and detailed.
2. Highly developed or complicated in design or planning.

**Verb (intransitive and transitive)**
1. To develop or present a theory, policy, or system in further detail. *Could you elaborate on that point?*
2. To add more information or detail to something already said or written.
3. To produce or develop something with painstaking care and great detail.

## Etymology
From Latin *elaboratus*, past participle of *elaborare* ("to work out, produce by labor"), from *e-/ex-* ("out") + *laborare* ("to labor, work"), from *labor* ("work, toil"). Entered English in the late 16th century.

## Usage Examples
1. "The Aztecs developed an **elaborate** system of aqueducts and canals to supply fresh water to the city of Tenochtitlan." — *Smithsonian Magazine*
2. "The professor asked the student to **elaborate** on her thesis regarding the causes of the Industrial Revolution."
3. "The researchers published an **elaborate** analysis of the genetic factors contributing to the disease." — *Nature*
4. "He declined to **elaborate** on the reasons for his resignation during the press conference." — *The Washington Post*

## Synonyms & Antonyms
**Synonyms (adjective):** intricate, detailed, complex, ornate, sophisticated, involved
**Synonyms (verb):** expand, expound, develop, explain, amplify, clarify, flesh out
**Antonyms (adjective):** simple, plain, basic, unadorned, straightforward
**Antonyms (verb):** summarize, condense, simplify, abbreviate

## TOEFL Tips
- One of the most versatile TOEFL words because it functions as both an adjective and a verb with different pronunciations.
- As a **verb**, it commonly appears in speaking and writing prompts: "Elaborate on the reasons why..."
- As an **adjective**, it appears in reading passages describing complex systems, architectures, or processes in history, biology, or sociology.
- In the **integrated speaking and writing** sections, you may need to "elaborate" on points from a lecture or reading, so understanding the verb form is essential.
- Note the pronunciation difference: the adjective ends in /-ət/ while the verb ends in /-eɪt/.
